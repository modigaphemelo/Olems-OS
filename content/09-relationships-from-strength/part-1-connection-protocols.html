Connection Protocols

Establishing secure channels. Starting with a firewall, then opening ports selectively.

The Handshake Protocol

In networking, before any data transfer happens, systems perform a handshake: they exchange credentials, establish encryption, agree on protocols, and test the connection. Most human connections skip this entirely—we jump straight to data transfer (intimacy, vulnerability, commitment) without establishing security first.

Connection Protocols are the human handshake.
They establish:
What level of access is being requested?
What security measures are in place?
What protocols will govern this connection?
What are the terms of data exchange?
No connection without protocol.
No data transfer without security.

The default approach is dangerous: we give everyone root access to our emotional core, then wonder why we get hacked. Connection protocols start everyone at firewall-level access, then gradually open ports based on demonstrated trustworthiness.

The Four Connection Levels

Level 1: Public Access

The website layer. What you share with everyone: professional bio, public social media, general interests. This is your public-facing interface. Anyone can access this without authentication.

Level 2: Authenticated Access

The user account layer. What you share with acquaintances: personal interests, opinions, casual conversation. Requires basic authentication (mutual introduction, shared context). Limited data exchange.

Level 3: Privileged Access

The API layer. What you share with friends: vulnerabilities, struggles, dreams, deeper values. Requires significant authentication (demonstrated trust, reciprocity). Controlled data exchange with clear boundaries.

Level 4: Root Access

The SSH layer. What you share with intimate partners/family: core trauma, deepest fears, ultimate dreams. Requires maximum authentication (years of demonstrated trust, unconditional positive regard). Extreme caution required.

The Trust Gradient Protocol

How to escalate connection levels safely.

1

Initial Handshake

Exchange public keys. Share public-level information first: name, basic background, context of meeting. Observe how they handle this basic exchange. Do they respect boundaries? Are they reciprocal?

2

Small Data Test

Send small, low-risk data. Share a minor opinion, preference, or harmless vulnerability. Watch how they handle it: do they dismiss it? exploit it? reciprocate? respect it? This tests their data handling protocols.

3

Boundary Test

Set a small boundary. Say no to something minor. Express a different opinion. See how they respond to disagreement or limitation. Do they respect boundaries or try to bypass them?

4

Reciprocity Check

Observe energy exchange. Is the connection reciprocal? Do they share as much as you share? Do they initiate as much as you initiate? One-way connections are security vulnerabilities.

5

Gradual Escalation

Open ports slowly. Only after passing previous tests do you escalate to the next level. Each level requires passing that level's tests. No skipping levels. No fast-tracking intimacy.

Connection Security Indicators

What to monitor during the handshake phase.

Red Flags (Security Threats)

  • Boundary Testing: Ignores or challenges small boundaries
  • Reciprocity Failure: Takes but doesn't give
  • Fast Intimacy: Pushes for deep sharing immediately
  • Disrespect: Dismisses your values or preferences
  • Entitlement: Acts like they deserve access they haven't earned
  • Impatience: Gets frustrated with gradual escalation

Green Flags (Security Assets)

  • Boundary Respect: Honors even unstated boundaries
  • Reciprocity: Matches your level of sharing
  • Patience: Comfortable with gradual progression
  • Curiosity: Asks questions, shows genuine interest
  • Vulnerability: Shares appropriately for connection level
  • Consistency: Behavior matches words over time

The Intimacy Bypass Attack

Some people (consciously or unconsciously) try to bypass security protocols by sharing deep vulnerabilities immediately. This creates false intimacy and pressure to reciprocate. It's an emotional DDoS attack. The proper response: acknowledge respectfully but don't reciprocate at that level. "Thank you for sharing that. It sounds like that was really difficult for you." Then continue at the appropriate level.

This Week's Connection Protocol Practice

Connection Security Audit

  1. Current Connections Audit: List your 10 closest relationships. What level of access does each have? Was it earned through protocol or given freely?
  2. New Connection Protocol: With one new acquaintance this week, consciously follow the 5-step trust gradient. Document the experience.
  3. Boundary Test: In an existing relationship, set one small new boundary. Observe and document the response.
  4. Access Review: Identify one relationship where access level may be too high for demonstrated trust. Consider downgrading.
  5. Protocol Writing: Write your personal connection protocol: what tests must someone pass to reach each level?

Connection protocols aren't about being cold or distrustful. They're about being intelligent and secure. You wouldn't give a stranger your bank password. Don't give them your emotional password either. Trust must be earned through demonstrated behavior, not assumed from initial impressions.
