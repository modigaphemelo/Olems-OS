Daily Workflows

The production pipeline of your day. Moving work from intake to completion.

The Workflow Principle

A workflow is not a to-do list. It's a systematic process that moves work from input to output with minimal friction. Like an assembly line in a factory, each station has a specific function, and work flows smoothly from one to the next. Most people's days are disorganized workshops where they constantly search for tools.

Daily Workflows are the pipelines of your productivity.
Intake → Processing → Execution → Review.
Email becomes handled communication.
Ideas become executed projects.
Problems become solved challenges.
Chaos becomes managed progress.

The goal isn't to work harder. It's to design workflows so intelligent that work flows effortlessly through them. Your cognitive energy should go to the work itself, not to figuring out how to do the work.

The Four Workflow Stations

Station 1: Intake & Triage

Where work enters the system. Email, messages, requests, ideas, tasks. This station captures everything, categorizes it, and routes it to the appropriate next station. Nothing gets lost; everything gets processed.

Station 2: Processing & Planning

Where work gets organized. Clarifying what needs to be done, estimating effort, assigning priority, scheduling time. This is where vague "stuff" becomes specific "next actions."

Station 3: Execution & Focus

Where work actually gets done. Deep work blocks, focused sessions, batch processing. This station is protected from interruptions and optimized for flow state. Work enters, value emerges.

Station 4: Review & Completion

Where work gets finished properly. Quality checking, documentation, communication of completion, archiving. This station ensures work is truly done, not just abandoned at 90%.

The Daily Workflow Rhythm

Different times of day are optimal for different types of work.

Chaotic Rhythm (Most People)

  • Morning: Reactive email checking
  • Mid-morning: Meetings interrupting work
    • Afternoon: Trying to focus while tired
  • Late afternoon: Administrative catch-up
  • Result: Constant context switching, low deep work

Designed Rhythm (Workflow Architecture)

  • 8-10am: Deep Work Block (Creative/Strategic)
  • 10-11am: Communication Block (Email/Meetings)
  • 11-1pm: Deep Work Block (Execution)
  • 1-2pm: Break & Learning
  • 2-4pm: Administrative Block (Processing)
  • 4-5pm: Planning & Shutdown
  • Result: Protected focus, managed communication

The Ultradian Rhythm

Your brain naturally works in 90-120 minute cycles of focus followed by 20-30 minute recovery. The workflow rhythm aligns with this biology: 90 minutes of deep work → 30 minutes of administrative/communication work → repeat. Fighting this rhythm creates burnout; flowing with it creates sustainable productivity.

The Focus Block Protocol

How to structure your deep work sessions for maximum output.

1

Preparation (5 minutes)

Set up the environment. Clear desk, close unnecessary tabs, set timer, gather materials, put phone in another room. Physical preparation precedes mental preparation.

2

Priming (2 minutes)

State setting. Deep breath, state your intention ("I will complete this section"), visualize successful completion. Mental preparation precedes work.

3

Execution (90 minutes)

Uninterrupted work. No checking email, no social media, no "quick" diversions. If ideas for other things come up, capture them on a notepad and return to work.

4

Recovery (20 minutes)

Mandatory break. Stand up, move, hydrate, look at something distant. This isn't "wasting time"—it's neural consolidation and preparation for the next focus block.

The Two Types of Work

Deep Work: Cognitively demanding, creates new value, requires uninterrupted focus, moves you forward. Examples: writing, coding, designing, strategizing.

Shallow Work: Logistical, administrative, communicative, necessary but not cognitively demanding. Examples: email, meetings, scheduling, reporting.

Most people fill their days with shallow work while complaining they have no time for deep work. Workflow architecture reverses this: schedule deep work first, protect it religiously, then fit shallow work around it.

Batch Processing: The Secret to Efficiency

Similar tasks done together are done faster and better.

This Week's Workflow Design

  1. Work Audit: Track your work for 2 days. Categorize each activity as Deep Work or Shallow Work.
  2. Rhythm Design: Create your ideal daily workflow rhythm based on your energy patterns.
  3. Focus Block Implementation: Schedule and protect at least one 90-minute focus block daily for 5 days.
  4. Batch Identification: Identify 3 types of shallow work you can batch process (e.g., email only at 10am and 3pm).
  5. Interruption Protocol: Create a "do not disturb" system for focus blocks (sign, app blockers, auto-responder).

A well-designed workflow feels like water flowing downhill—effortless, natural, powerful. It's not about working more hours; it's about designing hours that work better.
