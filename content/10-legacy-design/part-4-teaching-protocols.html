The Expertise Trap

Experts often suffer from the "curse of knowledge" - they know something so well they forget what it's like not to know it. Their teaching assumes background they no longer remember learning. They share complexity instead of creating clarity.

The Teaching Insight

Great teaching isn't about showing how much you know. It's about creating pathways for others to know it too. The measure of teaching effectiveness isn't the teacher's knowledge - it's the student's understanding.

The Teaching Protocols Framework

Concept Layering

Building understanding in progressive layers. Start with the core mental model, then add complexity. Each layer builds on the previous, never assuming knowledge that hasn't been taught.

Translation Protocols

Converting expert language into learner language. Taking complex concepts and expressing them in simple, relatable terms. The art of saying profound things plainly.

Application Bridges

Creating immediate pathways from theory to practice. Every concept should have a "do this now" component. Knowledge that isn't applied is entertainment, not education.

Feedback Integration

Systems for learning from teaching. Every teaching session generates data about what works and what doesn't. Great teachers are perpetual students of their own teaching.

Building Your Teaching System

1

Knowledge Deconstruction

Break down what you know into its component parts. What are the foundational concepts? What builds on what? Create a dependency map of your knowledge.

Knowledge Map Template
// TEACHING DEPENDENCY MAP Core Concept: [The fundamental idea] Prerequisites: [What must be understood first] Supporting Concepts: [Related ideas that help] Applications: [Practical uses] Common Misconceptions: [Where people get stuck] Advanced Extensions: [Where this leads next]
2

Learning Pathway Design

Design the optimal sequence for learning. Start with what's most valuable, not what's most fundamental. Create multiple entry points for different learning styles.

The Feynman Protocol

1. Choose a concept
2. Explain it to a 12-year-old
3. Identify gaps in your explanation
4. Simplify and create analogies
5. Repeat until crystal clear

3

Teaching Artifact Creation

Create the actual teaching materials. These become your teaching legacy - the artifacts that can teach even when you're not present.

Frameworks

Mental models that organize thinking. The 2x2 matrix, the checklist, the decision tree. Frameworks scale thinking.

Step-by-Step Guides

Clear procedures that anyone can follow. Recipes for success that remove ambiguity and guesswork.

Common Q&A

Anticipated questions with clear answers. The FAQ that prevents people from getting stuck.

Expert Communication vs. Effective Teaching

Dimension Expert Communication Effective Teaching
Goal Demonstrate expertise Create understanding
Language Technical jargon Simple analogies
Structure Logical to expert Progressive for learner
Assumptions Prior knowledge exists No prior knowledge assumed
Success Measure "That was impressive" "Now I can do it myself"
Output Information delivered Capability created

This Week's Implementation

Day 1

Choose Your Teaching Topic

Select one thing you know well that others would benefit from learning. Something specific enough to teach deeply, broad enough to be valuable.

Day 2

Create the Knowledge Map

Deconstruct your topic using the Knowledge Map Template. Identify core concepts, prerequisites, applications, and common sticking points.

Day 3

Feynman Protocol Practice

Explain your core concept to an imaginary 12-year-old. Record yourself. Listen back. Where did you assume knowledge? Where was it unclear? Refine.

Day 4-7

Create Teaching Artifact

Build one complete teaching artifact: a framework, a step-by-step guide, or a comprehensive Q&A. Make it something that could teach without you.

Teaching as Learning

The process of preparing to teach will deepen your own understanding. You'll discover gaps in your knowledge, unclear thinking, and assumptions you didn't know you had. Teaching isn't just giving - it's improving.
